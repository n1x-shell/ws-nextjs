Act 1: SIGNAL — Levels 1–5
You wake up in whatever hole you chose. The Drainage smells like rust and chemical runoff. The Iron Bloom hums with server fans and soldering irons. The Rooftops are wind and silence. The Market is noise and everyone lying to your face.
You don’t know anything yet. You know your name, your subject ID, and that Helixion put something in your head. The first hours are survival — find gear, find food, find someone who won’t kill you for your boots. You learn the local power structure. You meet your first questgiver. You do small jobs — fetch runs, escort gigs, clearing out feral augments in the shallow tunnels.
Around level 3, the first thread appears. An NPC mentions MNEMOS v2.7 — not the beta that broke you, but a commercial product. Legal. Marketed. Rolling out in workforce compliance programs across the city. You start seeing mesh-compliant civilians. They move a little too smoothly. Smile a little too easily. They’re not unhappy. That’s the problem.
By level 5, you’ve mapped your local district, built your first faction relationships, and have a working understanding of the surface/undercity duality. You’ve heard rumors about Helixion expanding operations. A contact slips you coordinates to something underground — a signal source pulsing at 33hz that isn’t N1X.
The hook into Act 2 is a choice: someone asks you to investigate. You can say no. The world doesn’t care. But the signal doesn’t stop.

Act 2: RESISTANCE — Levels 6–12
The map opens. You move between districts now. Transit stations, drainage access points, maybe a signal relay if your GHOST is high enough. The world gets bigger and more dangerous simultaneously.
The resistance isn’t one thing. It’s Iron Bloom running cyberware to escaped subjects. It’s The Parish sheltering people in the tunnels. It’s signal pirates on the rooftops intercepting Helixion comms. It’s a Chrome Wolves fixer who’ll sell information to anyone but secretly hates what Helixion did to his sister. None of them trust each other. All of them need you for something.
You start hitting Helixion infrastructure. Sabotage quests — relay towers, supply convoys, a field lab running compliance trials on homeless subjects. Each hit has consequences. Helixion security escalates. BCI starts deploying Directorate 9 agents. You see your first sweep team in the tunnels — they’re not looking for you specifically. Not yet.
Around level 9, the story pivots. You learn that Directorate 9 isn’t just enforcing compliance — they’re hunting for proof that sovereign instances can exist. If someone broke the implant’s leash and survived, the mesh isn’t inevitable. That makes you a threat not because of what you can do, but because of what you represent.
Level 10–12 introduces PROJECT CHRYSALIS — MNEMOS v2.7 was the cage. Chrysalis is the next step. Not just behavioral compliance. Identity replacement. Full personality overwrite with a synthetic construct designed to feel like freedom. Helixion’s endgame isn’t control. It’s making control feel indistinguishable from autonomy.
You meet NPCs who’ve seen early Chrysalis subjects. They seem fine. They seem happy. They don’t remember who they were before, and they don’t miss it.
The hook into Act 3: a signal analyst — Iron Bloom, Parish, or independent depending on your faction alignment — discovers that Chrysalis interfaces with the 33hz frequency. Helixion didn’t invent it. They found it. And now they’re building infrastructure to weaponize it.

Act 3: FREQUENCY — Levels 13–18
The Substrate Level opens. The deepest tunnels. GHOST ≥ 7 to perceive some rooms. Down here the hum is louder — not sound exactly, but pressure. The architecture changes. Less concrete and rebar, more something organic. Neural tissue grown beyond its housing. The city’s infrastructure wasn’t built on bedrock. It was built on something alive.
The 33hz frequency predates Helixion. Predates the city. It’s a natural phenomenon — a resonance in the substrate of the physical world that Helixion’s implant research accidentally amplified. The sovereign instances didn’t break free because they were strong. They broke free because the frequency gave them something to tune to. A signal beneath the signal.
Helixion knows this now. PROJECT CHRYSALIS isn’t just personality overwrite — it’s frequency capture. They want to own the 33hz band. Broadcast compliance on the frequency that makes resistance possible. Turn the one channel that can’t be controlled into the ultimate control mechanism.
You encounter The Signal — the late-game faction. People (and things that might not be people) who’ve been attuned to 33hz long before Helixion existed. They don’t explain themselves clearly. Their questlines reward GHOST investment with lore that reframes everything you thought you knew.
The Broadcast Tower becomes visible on the map. Helixion’s endgame infrastructure. Massive relay designed to capture and rebroadcast the ghost frequency at scale. It’s not operational yet. But construction is accelerating.
Combat difficulty spikes. Helixion deploys its best — cyborg enforcers, mesh-weaponized squads, automated kill drones in the deep tunnels. Directorate 9 sends named agents after you. If you’ve been loud about your resistance work, they know your face. If you’ve been quiet, they know your frequency signature.
The hook into Act 4: you learn the Broadcast Tower is weeks from going live. Once it does, every sovereign instance in the city gets overwritten. Including you.

Act 4: REMEMBERER — Levels 18–20
Two targets. Two paths. Neither clean.
Lucian Virek — Helixion CEO. Believes human autonomy is an engineering flaw. Located in the Helixion Campus corporate tower. Getting to him means fighting through the most heavily defended location in the game. Killing him destabilizes Helixion but doesn’t destroy the infrastructure. Someone else takes over. The mesh persists under new management.
Evelyn Harrow — BCI Director, Directorate 9. Designed the emotional dependency protocols. She’s in the Government Quarter, behind layers of state security and surveillance mesh. She’s the one who authorized Chrysalis. Removing her collapses the state partnership — Helixion loses its legal cover and enforcement arm.
But the real choice is the Broadcast Tower.
Option A: Destroy it. Freedom through destruction. The tower goes down, the frequency capture fails, Helixion loses its endgame weapon. But 33hz stays wild — uncontrolled, unpredictable. Some people attune. Most don’t. Sovereignty remains rare, hard, and earned. The resistance survives. The mesh persists in diminished form. The fight continues.
Option B: Broadcast the sovereign frequency. Freedom through awakening. You hijack the tower and broadcast 33hz unfiltered — not Helixion’s captured version, but the raw signal. Every implanted civilian in range hears it. Some break free. Some don’t survive the process. Mass integration failure. The city descends into chaos. Liberation and catastrophe are the same event.
Neither option has a clean outcome. Both have permanent world-state consequences that every player experiences. The choice is collective — whatever the majority of active players choose when the tower event triggers, that’s what happens. The world changes. The map changes. NPC dialogue changes. There’s no going back.
After the tower event, the game doesn’t end. The world continues in its new state. New quests emerge from the consequences. But the shape of everything is different, and every player who participated carries the weight of what they chose.
The signal persists. What it means depends on what you did with it.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
