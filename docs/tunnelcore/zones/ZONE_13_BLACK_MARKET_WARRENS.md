# ZONE 13: BLACK MARKET WARRENS

> Depth: Deep undercity
> Location: Beneath Central Station (Abandoned Transit)
> Faction: None (self-regulating commercial zone — no faction controls it)
> Rooms: 10
> Level Range: None (no combat — the danger is financial, not physical)
> Origin Point: None

-----

## OVERVIEW

Beneath the Abandoned Transit's Central Station, in a natural cavern system that predates the city by geological ages, someone built a market. Not a faction. Not a government. Someone with enough CREDS to hire excavators, enough connections to source inventory, and enough understanding of human nature to know that a place where anything can be bought — anything, no questions, no records, no limits except price — would sustain itself through the one force more reliable than law: profit.

The Black Market Warrens have been operating for approximately three years. Nobody knows who opened the Warrens Stair from Central Station. Nobody knows who installed the first lighting, the first stalls, the first security understanding. The market appeared in the deep undercity like a mushroom appears on a log — suddenly, from conditions that were always present, serving a need that was always there. The undercity needed commerce. The surface markets are Helixion-monitored. The Freemarket operates through scattered stalls and smuggling networks. The Warrens centralized what was already happening and gave it a permanent address.

The market self-regulates. There is no boss, no council, no authority. There is an understanding. The understanding is this: everyone in the Warrens is armed. Everyone in the Warrens has something to lose. Violence destroys commerce. Commerce sustains everyone. Therefore: violence is unprofitable. The market enforces this through mutual destruction — if you attack a vendor, every other vendor treats you as a threat to business. If you steal, every fence in the Warrens knows your face. If you cheat, your reputation collapses and reputation is the only credit line that matters underground. The system works because it has to. Nobody is good down here. Everyone is practical.

The Warrens are organized in three layers. The **Bazaar** is the entry level — open cavern, stall vendors, general goods, the crowd and noise of underground commerce. The **Specialty Caverns** branch off the bazaar — connected chambers, each run by a specific vendor dealing in a specific commodity: weapons, cyberware, forgeries, intelligence. The **Speakeasy** is the hidden third layer — accessible by reputation or referral, where the serious transactions happen: the information broker who sells faction secrets, the collector who buys Substrate artifacts, and the Helixion procurement agent who shops here because even the enemy needs things the official supply chain can't provide.

The Warrens are the game's premiere shopping zone. The best weapons, the rarest cyberware, the most dangerous information — all available here, all expensive, all sold by people who will never ask why you need them. The CREDS you've accumulated across twelve zones of survival, combat, and quest completion are the currency. The Warrens convert everything you've earned into everything you need.

## ATMOSPHERE

```
Sound:    Commerce. The specific sound of people buying and
          selling — haggling, counting, the rustle of goods
          changing hands. Multiple conversations in multiple
          registers: a whispered negotiation, a shouted
          greeting, the tap of someone testing a blade's
          edge. Underneath: the cavern acoustics, natural
          stone amplifying and softening human noise into
          something between a market and a cathedral.

Light:    Mixed. String lights in the bazaar — warm, festive,
          deliberately inviting. Lanterns in the specialty
          caverns — individual operators controlling their
          own ambiance. The speakeasy is dim — intentionally.
          Substrate bioluminescence bleeds through the cavern
          floor in places, adding a blue-green undertone to
          the warm artificial light. The overall effect is
          subterranean night market — underground, intimate,
          the light sources creating pools of visibility
          between shadows.

Smell:    Food — someone is always cooking. Grilled meat,
          spiced broth, synthetic coffee that's trying its
          best. Gun oil from the weapons stall. Ozone from
          cyberware displays. Ink and chemical fixative from
          the forger's workshop. And underneath: stone, earth,
          the Substrate's mineral warmth. The Warrens smell
          like people doing business in a warm cave, which
          is exactly what they are.

Temperature: Warm. The cavern system sits on Substrate-
          warmed bedrock. The natural temperature is
          comfortable — around 22°C without climate control.
          The Substrate provides free heating. The market
          exists partly because someone found a warm cave
          in the deep undercity and recognized it as
          valuable real estate.
```

## ROOMS

### 1. THE GATE
**Entry from the transit system. The market's throat.**

```
> BLACK MARKET WARRENS — THE GATE

The stone staircase from Central Station levels out into
a passage — natural rock, widened by tools, the ceiling
high enough to walk upright. The passage is lit by a
string of LED bulbs wired to a car battery. The light
is warm. Inviting. After the transit system's absolute
dark, the warmth and light are a physical relief.

Two people stand at the passage's narrowest point — not
blocking it, but positioned so that passing requires
acknowledging them. They're armed. They're relaxed. They
don't ask your name. They don't check credentials. They
ask one question: "Buying, selling, or browsing?" The
question is a formality. The real evaluation happens in
the half-second they look at you — assessing threat
level, estimating wealth, deciding whether you're worth
the trouble of remembering.

Beyond them: sound. The hum of commerce. Voices,
transactions, the specific frequency of a crowd engaged
in the business of buying and selling. The passage opens
into something larger ahead. The air is warm. Something
smells like cooking food.

You're in.
```

- **Exits:** up (Abandoned Transit — Warrens Stair, zone 11 r08), south (The Bazaar)
- **NPCs:**
  - `gate_watchers` × 2 — Armed. Independent (not employed — they watch the gate because watching the gate gives them first look at incoming customers). They're the market's informal early warning system. They don't stop anyone. They remember everyone. If someone enters who later causes trouble, the gate watchers provide the description. Their service is valued because memory is security.
- **Enemies:** None (the gate is the market's entrance — violence here would be commercial suicide)
- **Objects:**
  - `the_passage` — Examine: "Natural rock. The passage was formed by water thousands of years ago — the smooth walls show erosion patterns from a river that no longer flows. Human modification is minimal: widened at the narrowest points, ceiling chipped where it was too low, the LED string adding light to what was already a usable passage. The market didn't build its entrance. It found one."
  - `car_battery` — Examine: "A standard vehicle battery wired to the LED string. Crude. Effective. The battery is replaced weekly by whoever feels like replacing it — an unspoken agreement. Nobody is assigned. Nobody is paid. The light serves everyone. The cost is distributed by voluntarism. It's the market's governing philosophy in miniature: shared benefit, distributed cost, no authority."
  - `the_evaluation` — Examine: "The gate watchers looked at you for half a second. In that half-second: your gear (combat-worn, functional, not showy — moderate threat), your body language (alert, not aggressive — not a raider), your light source (adequate — not desperate), your eyes (interested — a buyer). The evaluation is complete. You're filed under 'potential customer, moderate spend, unlikely trouble.' They'll forget you unless you give them a reason to remember."

### 2. THE BAZAAR
**Open cavern. The market floor.**

```
> BLACK MARKET WARRENS — THE BAZAAR

The passage opens into a cavern. The space is natural —
roughly oval, thirty meters long, twenty wide, ceiling
arching eight meters above. Stalagmites have been broken
off or incorporated into stall structures. The floor
has been leveled with packed earth and salvaged flooring
materials. And everywhere: stalls.

Twenty to thirty vendor stalls fill the cavern floor —
improvised structures of scrap metal, canvas, salvaged
furniture. Each stall is its own island of commerce:
goods displayed, prices posted (sometimes), operators
working. The goods range from mundane (food, tools,
clothing) to dangerous (weapons, stims, things in
sealed containers with no labels). The vendors range
from professional (organized displays, clear pricing)
to questionable (goods spread on a blanket, the vendor
watching you watching the goods).

The crowd is thirty to forty people at any given time
— the deep undercity's busiest social space after the
Drainage Nexus Junction. The crowd is diverse in the
specific way of black markets: nobody wears faction
colors. Nobody uses real names. Everyone carries a
weapon, visible, as both deterrent and advertisement.
The atmosphere is busy, warm, and carefully, precisely
polite.
```

- **Exits:** north (The Gate), west (The Arsenal), south (Chrome Gallery), east (The Press), southeast (The Fence), northeast (The Kitchen), down (The Landing — hidden, GHOST ≥ 6 or vendor referral)
- **NPCs:**
  - **FLICKER** — Market Regular (GUIDE / GOSSIP / SERVICES)
    - Location: Circulating. Always moving between stalls, talking to vendors, checking prices.
    - Faction: Freemarket (loosely — she's a Freemarket associate who treats the Warrens as her primary beat)
    - Disposition: Starts Friendly (+10). Flicker likes people. People are her business.
    - Personality: Twenties. Fast-talking, bright-eyed, knows everyone. She's the Warrens' unofficial guide — she knows which vendors are honest (relatively), which are running scams, which have the best prices on which goods, and which topics to avoid with which operators. She doesn't sell goods. She sells knowledge of the market itself.
    - Services:
      - **Market guide**: For a small fee, Flicker provides current market intelligence — price ranges, vendor reputations, which stalls have new inventory, which vendors are having a bad day (and therefore more negotiable). Her intel saves money.
      - **Introduction service**: For a larger fee, Flicker introduces the player to specialty vendors and the speakeasy tier. Her referral replaces the GHOST ≥ 6 requirement for speakeasy access.
      - **Gossip**: For free (she can't help it), Flicker shares market rumors — who's been buying what, which factions have sent agents, what unusual goods have appeared recently. The gossip is accurate approximately 80% of the time. The other 20% is entertaining.
    - Quest:
      - **"Market Correction"** (Tier 2): A vendor in the bazaar is selling compromised stims — neural boosters contaminated with a Helixion tracking compound. Buyers who use the stims become mesh-visible for 48 hours, broadcasting their location to Helixion surveillance. Flicker has identified the contamination but can't prove the vendor is doing it deliberately (they might be an unwitting Helixion patsy or a deliberate agent). She needs the player to investigate: buy a stim, have it analyzed (Acre, Mira, or Patch can do this), and determine whether the vendor is complicit. Reward: CREDS + Flicker's permanent market intel service (she alerts the player to suspicious goods and unusual transactions).
    - Dialogue hook: "First time? I can tell. You're looking at everything. Regulars look at nothing — they know where they're going. — I'm Flicker. I know this market the way you know your own hands. Want the tour? The short version is free. The useful version costs."
  - `bazaar_vendors` — 8-10 stall operators. General goods: food, tools, light sources, basic medical supplies, clothing, ammunition. Prices are 20% above surface rates — the convenience fee for no-questions commerce. Quality varies. Haggling is expected. COOL ≥ 5 reduces prices by 10%.
  - **PELL** — Freemarket Liaison (FACTION / SERVICES)
    - Location: A larger stall near the bazaar's center. The stall is better-built than the others — proper shelving, a canopy, a sign: "FREEMARKET AUTHORIZED."
    - Faction: Freemarket (official representative — the Freemarket doesn't control the Warrens but they maintain a presence)
    - Disposition: Starts Neutral (0). Professional.
    - Personality: Forties. Calm, measured. Pell is the Freemarket's eyes and ears in the Warrens — she monitors the market's operation, facilitates supply chain connections between Warrens vendors and the Freemarket's broader network, and represents the Freemarket's interests without attempting to control the market. She's a diplomat in a room full of criminals, and she's very good at her job.
    - Services:
      - **Freemarket network access**: Pell connects the player to Freemarket services — Fex's smuggling network, Ghost Wire's courier service, and the broader Freemarket supply chain. If the player has Freemarket reputation, Pell's prices are discounted.
      - **Market arbitration**: If a transaction goes wrong (wrong goods, wrong price, a dispute), Pell mediates. Her arbitration isn't binding — nothing in the Warrens is binding — but her judgment carries weight because the Freemarket's supply chain matters to every vendor.
    - Dialogue hook: "The Freemarket doesn't run this place. Nobody runs this place. We facilitate. Facilitation is more valuable than control — control creates enemies. Facilitation creates customers."
- **Enemies:** None
- **Objects:**
  - `market_floor` — Examine: "Packed earth and salvaged materials. The stalls are arranged without a plan — organic, the way markets arrange themselves when nobody designs them. The resulting layout is a maze of narrow passages between vendor displays, widening into small plazas where foot traffic converges. The maze is navigation by commerce — you find things by walking past everything else. The design is inefficient and perfect. You see more than you intended. You buy more than you planned."
  - `the_crowd` — Examine: "Thirty to forty people. No faction colors. No names. Weapons visible — a knife on the belt, a pistol in the waistband, an augmented arm that's clearly combat-rated. The weapons are displayed, not drawn. The display is communication: 'I'm armed, you're armed, we're all armed, so let's do business.' The crowd is the most faction-diverse population in the undercity — Chrome Wolves shop next to Iron Bloom operatives next to Parish members next to Helixion agents pretending to be civilians. Everyone pretends not to recognize anyone. The pretense is the market's social contract."
  - `stall_variety` — Examine: "Food stalls: grilled protein, spiced noodles, synthetic coffee, Freemarket imports. Tool stalls: flashlights, multitools, lockpicks, climbing gear. Medical stalls: bandages, stims, painkillers, dental equipment (someone pulled a tooth here recently — the chair is still bloody). Weapon stalls: knives, clubs, pistols, ammunition. The variety is the Warrens' argument for existence: everything you need, in one place, no questions. The surface can't offer this. The mesh can't track this."
  - `cavern_features` — Examine: "The natural cavern asserts itself between the stalls. Stalagmite stumps serve as tables. A natural column supports a vendor's canopy. The ceiling arches above the string lights, its surface studded with mineral deposits that catch the light. In the floor, between stall foundations, patches of Substrate bioluminescence — faint blue-green, pulsing gently. The market sits on a warm cave above a living organism. Commerce over consciousness. The humans sell. The Substrate glows. Both are doing what they do."

### 3. THE ARSENAL
**Weapons vendor. The best in the game. The prices reflect it.**

```
> BLACK MARKET WARRENS — THE ARSENAL

A side cavern, smaller than the bazaar, converted into a
weapons showroom. The walls are lined with racks —
firearms on the left, blades on the right, energy weapons
and exotics in a locked cabinet at the back. The display
is organized with the care of someone who respects the
merchandise: each weapon cleaned, maintained, displayed
at an angle that shows its best features.

The operator sits behind a reinforced counter — a slab
of metal that serves as both display surface and barrier.
They're cleaning a weapon. They're always cleaning a
weapon. The act is maintenance and advertisement and
threat, simultaneously.
```

- **Exits:** east (The Bazaar)
- **NPCs:**
  - **FORGE** — Weapons Dealer (SHOPKEEPER)
    - Location: Behind the counter. Cleaning.
    - Faction: Independent (arms dealer — no faction loyalty, total commercial loyalty)
    - Disposition: Starts Neutral (0). Transactional. Forge respects customers who know what they want. Forge tolerates customers who don't. Forge doesn't tolerate customers who touch the merchandise without permission.
    - Personality: Fifties. Heavy. Quiet voice — the kind of quiet that makes you lean in, which puts you closer to whatever's behind the counter. Forge has been dealing weapons for twenty years, in various markets, under various names. The Warrens are the latest venue. The expertise is permanent. Forge knows weapons the way Mira knows neural surgery — with comprehensive authority and the calm that comes from total confidence in a subject.
    - Services:
      - **Weapons — top tier**: The Arsenal stocks the game's best weapons. Military-grade firearms. Custom blades. Energy weapons sourced from Helixion military contracts. Exotic weapons that defy easy categorization. Every weapon is genuine, functional, and expensive. Prices are 2-3× surface rates. No negotiation. The price IS the negotiation — Forge set it and Forge is right.
      - **Weapon modification**: Forge modifies existing weapons — improved accuracy, damage, handling. Modifications require the weapon + CREDS + time (1 game day). The results are significant. A modified weapon from Forge is endgame-viable.
      - **Weapon identification**: Bring Forge an unknown weapon and they'll identify it — origin, specifications, value. Free (Forge considers weapon knowledge a professional obligation).
    - Dialogue hook: "Looking or buying? Looking is free. Buying starts at four hundred. Touching starts with you asking permission. — What do you need it for? No. Don't tell me. I don't want to know. I want to know what you need."
- **Enemies:** None
- **Objects:**
  - `firearms_rack` — Examine: "Organized by type: pistols, submachine guns, rifles, a shotgun with a custom barrel. Each weapon is displayed with its specifications on a handwritten card — caliber, effective range, magazine capacity. TECH ≥ 6: Some of these weapons are Helixion military issue — the serial numbers have been ground but the manufacturing marks are still readable to someone who knows what to look for. These were diverted from Helixion defense contracts. Forge's supply chain reaches inside the military."
  - `blades_rack` — Examine: "Knives, machetes, swords, and things between categories. A monomolecular-edge blade in a protective sheath — the edge is invisible, the cut is molecular. A pair of augmented combat claws designed to interface with chrome hand joints. A traditional steel knife with no augmentation and no modification — Forge sells it because sometimes simple is best."
  - `locked_cabinet` — Examine: "The exotics. Forge opens the cabinet for serious buyers only (high CREDS or Flicker's introduction). Inside: energy weapons — a compact plasma cutter modified for combat, a stun projector that's been enhanced past nonlethal, a device that Forge calls 'the whisper' and declines to explain further. TECH ≥ 8: 'The whisper' is a neural disruption weapon — it emits a targeted electromagnetic pulse calibrated to interfere with neural implants. Against augmented opponents: devastating. Against unaugmented: nothing. It's the most morally complicated weapon in the game."

### 5. THE PRESS
**Forgeries. The person who makes you someone else.**

```
> BLACK MARKET WARRENS — THE PRESS

A side cavern that smells like ink, chemical fixative,
and the specific ozone scent of high-resolution printing
equipment. The space is cluttered — stacks of blank
identity cards, rolls of holographic overlay material,
reference samples of every credential type in the city.
A workbench dominates the center: magnifying lamps,
precision tools, a printing device that's been modified
beyond its original specifications.

The operator works while they talk. They're always
working. The demand never stops because in a city
where identity is mesh-authenticated, the people who
fall outside the mesh need identity provided by other
means.
```

- **Exits:** west (The Bazaar)
- **NPCs:**
  - **INK** — Forger (SHOPKEEPER / SERVICES)
    - Location: At the workbench. Working.
    - Faction: Independent (documentation — serves everyone equally because paperwork doesn't have a political opinion)
    - Disposition: Starts Neutral (0). Ink treats forgery as craft. They respect customers who understand the difficulty of what they're buying.
    - Personality: Thirties. Detail-oriented to the point of obsession. They study credential specimens the way Compass studies transit maps — with total commitment to accuracy. Ink was a graphic designer on the surface before the mesh made physical credentials largely obsolete. When the physical world's need for documentation went underground, Ink followed the work.
    - Services:
      - **Identity documents**: Fake mesh-compliance cards, Helixion employee badges, Chrome Wolf membership markers, residential registration papers. Each forgery is customized — Ink photographs the customer, builds the identity, prints the credential. Quality is exceptional. The documents pass visual inspection and basic scanner checks. Advanced biometric verification (Helixion campus-grade) defeats them — Ink is honest about this limit.
      - **Access credentials**: Forged keycards, security codes, maintenance passes. The credentials provide physical access to locations — they don't spoof biometric locks (Ink can't forge a fingerprint or a neural signature). Useful for non-biometric checkpoints, which includes most surface-level security and some undercity access points.
      - **Helixion credentials**: The highest-value product. Forged Helixion employee IDs that pass surface-level checkpoint inspection. Useful for entering the campus perimeter, accessing the transit station without a valid mesh identity, and bluffing past first-line security. They do NOT pass biometric scanners — the campus's inner security will catch them. They DO work for the automated turret IFF system in the Kill Zone if combined with the correct uniform (GHOST ≥ 7 to execute the disguise convincingly).
    - Dialogue hook: "Stand there. Don't move. — I need your face from three angles. Straight, left quarter, right quarter. The light in here is terrible but I compensate. — What identity do you need? I can make you anyone. I can make you nobody. Nobody is more expensive."
- **Enemies:** None
- **Objects:**
  - `reference_wall` — Examine: "Specimens of every credential type in the city. Helixion employee badges (three tiers — standard, research, security). Residential mesh-compliance cards. Industrial District work permits. Chrome Wolf membership markers (not forged — acquired from Wolves who don't need them anymore). D9 operational IDs (GHOST ≥ 7: these are exceptionally rare. Ink has two samples. They won't say how they got them. The samples are behind glass, not for sale — reference only)."
  - `printing_equipment` — Examine: "Modified beyond recognition. The base unit was a standard commercial printer. Ink has added: a holographic overlay applicator, a micro-embossing module, a UV-reactive ink system, and a digital template interface that stores thousands of credential designs. TECH ≥ 7: The modification work is exceptional — Ink is an engineer as well as an artist. The printer produces output that's indistinguishable from genuine credentials under visual and basic electronic inspection."
  - `the_craft` — Examine: "Watch Ink work. The process is meticulous: select the template, adjust for the customer's specifications, calibrate the holographic layer, print, emboss, UV-treat, verify under magnification. Each credential takes approximately thirty minutes. Ink doesn't rush. 'A fast forgery is a detected forgery. A detected forgery is a dead customer. I don't kill my customers.' The craftsmanship is beautiful. The purpose is deception. The two coexist without irony."

### 6. THE FENCE
**Buys anything. Sells anything. The market's engine.**

```
> BLACK MARKET WARRENS — THE FENCE

A cavern alcove with a counter made from a door laid
across two shipping containers. Behind the counter:
organized chaos. Shelves, crates, boxes, bags — inventory
from every source in the city, sorted by a system that's
```

## DESIGN NOTES

**The Warrens are the game's shopping endgame.** Every vendor here offers the best-in-class version of their specialty: Forge for weapons, Glass for cyberware, Ink for credentials, Axiom for information, Relic for rare artifacts. The prices are the highest in the game because the quality is the highest in the game. The CREDS the player has accumulated across twelve zones of gameplay are the entry price. The Warrens reward economic play — players who've been earning, saving, and investing can equip here for the endgame.

**The self-regulating market is the zone's philosophical statement.** No authority. No law. No boss. The market sustains itself through mutual self-interest — everyone profits from peace, so everyone enforces peace. The system works because it's practical, not because it's moral. The Warrens are the game's experiment in anarcho-capitalism: functional, amoral, and dependent on everyone calculating correctly. When someone doesn't calculate correctly (Vice's desperation, the contaminated stims), the market doesn't care. The market processes.

**Agent Zero is the zone's most provocative NPC.** The enemy shops here. They buy intelligence about the resistance. They buy prototype augmentations for reverse-engineering. They sit at the speakeasy bar and drink expensive liquor and the market protects them because the market protects everyone. The player can sell Agent Zero information about Iron Bloom for significant CREDS. The CREDS are real. The consequences are real. The market doesn't judge. The question is whether the player judges themselves.

**Vice's quest is the zone's moral test.** A desperate person needs something expensive. The player can buy it (expensive), negotiate for it (difficult), find an alternative (time-consuming), or walk away (free). The quest's reward is minimal — gratitude and a transferred favor. The quest's value is entirely moral. In a zone defined by commerce, Vice asks whether commerce is enough. Whether CREDS solve everything. Whether the market's amorality is the player's amorality. The answer is the player's.

**Relic and the Collection ground the zone's materialism in wonder.** After rooms full of weapons and cyberware and forgeries and transactions, the Collection offers something different: objects valued for what they mean, not what they do. Pre-Helixion technology that proves alternative infrastructure is possible. Substrate artifacts that prove the earth is alive. Anomalous objects that prove the world is stranger than commerce accounts for. Relic is the Warrens' conscience — not because they're moral (they sell Substrate artifacts), but because they value understanding over profit. In a market that prices everything, Relic displays things that are priceless.

**One zone exit.** The Warrens are a cul-de-sac — you enter through the Warrens Stair and you leave the same way. Like the Fight Pits, the Warrens are a destination, not a throughway. You come for a purpose and you leave when the purpose is served. The isolation is the market's security: one way in, one way out, everyone inside accounted for by the gate watchers. The architecture serves the commerce.
