# ZONE 12: IRON BLOOM SERVER FARM

> Depth: Deep undercity
> Location: Beneath the Fringe/Industrial District border
> Faction: Iron Bloom (resistance headquarters)
> Rooms: 10
> Level Range: 10–14 (Acts 2–3)
> Origin Point: AUGMENTED (starts at the clinic, post-rescue)

-----

## OVERVIEW

The Iron Bloom Collective operates out of a decommissioned server farm fifty meters beneath the border between the Fringe Ruins and the Industrial District. The facility was built twenty years ago to house municipal data infrastructure — server racks, climate control, power distribution, communication backbone. When Helixion absorbed the city's digital infrastructure into the mesh, the server farm was decommissioned and sealed. The hardware was left in place because removing it was more expensive than forgetting it.

Dr. Kael Serrano found it. Serrano, a former Helixion neural interface researcher who defected after seeing the Chrysalis prototype data, needed a place deep enough to be invisible, wired enough to be functional, and large enough to build a resistance. The server farm was all three. Over five years, Serrano and a growing collective of engineers, surgeons, hackers, and refugees transformed the abandoned facility into Iron Bloom's headquarters — augmentation clinic, intelligence center, workshop, and sanctuary.

The facility's soul is sanctuary. People come to Iron Bloom broken — corporate kill-switches in their heads, Chrysalis-damaged neural tissue, augmentations installed without consent. They come because someone they trusted told them where to look. They come because the surface world has no room for people who refuse the mesh. Iron Bloom takes them in. The clinic removes what shouldn't be there and installs what should. The living quarters provide the first safe sleep some of these people have had in years. The resistance grows from healing — every person Iron Bloom repairs becomes a person who understands what Helixion does, who carries the evidence in their own body, who has a reason to fight.

But it's also a workshop commune — messy, creative, collaborative. Engineers argue about signal propagation over soldering stations. Hackers build counter-signal tools from salvaged server hardware. The data analyst's evidence boards cover entire walls. The facility hums with the energy of people building something, even if what they're building is still taking shape. Iron Bloom isn't a military organization. It's a collective of experts who agree on one thing: Helixion must be stopped. They disagree about everything else — methods, timeline, priorities, the role of violence. The disagreements are the organization's engine and its vulnerability.

And at the center of all of it, in a workshop that smells like solder and antiseptic: Serrano. Brilliant. Visionary. Dying. Five months ago, Serrano attempted to remove their own corporate kill-switch — the implant Helixion installs in high-level researchers, designed to terminate neural function if the subject is flagged for decommissioning. Serrano had removed dozens from others. They couldn't get someone else to do theirs — the surgery requires the patient to be conscious and guided, and Serrano is the only person who understands the kill-switch architecture well enough to navigate the extraction. So they did it themselves. In a mirror. With local anesthetic and the steady hands that have saved a hundred other people.

The extraction failed. Not completely — the kill-switch is disabled, the remote trigger can't reach Serrano. But the removal damaged the surrounding neural tissue. Serrano's left hand trembles. Their short-term memory gaps. They have headaches that last for days. The damage is progressive — the neural tissue around the extraction site is degrading. Serrano has, by their own estimate, eight to fourteen months before the degradation reaches critical cognitive function.

The resistance leader is dying. The work that Iron Bloom exists to do — freeing people from corporate neural control — is the work that's killing the person who started it. Serrano knows. The inner circle knows. The rank and file don't. The urgency of Iron Bloom's mission — stopping the Broadcast Tower before it activates — isn't just political. It's personal. Serrano needs to finish this before they can't think anymore.

## ATMOSPHERE

```
Sound:    Server hum — lower and older than the mesh frequency,
          the sound of legacy hardware running on refurbished
          power. Voices — more voices than any other undercity
          location. People working, talking, arguing, laughing.
          The clinic has its own sound: quiet conversation,
          the click of surgical tools, someone breathing
          through anesthesia. The workshop has another: the
          whine of soldering irons, the tap of keyboards, the
          occasional curse when something doesn't work.
          The living quarters have a third: music, cooking,
          the domestic sounds of people living together
          underground. The facility sounds alive in a way
          no other undercity zone does.

Light:    Full-spectrum LED arrays — installed by Iron Bloom
          to simulate natural daylight. After the transit
          system's darkness, the Iron Bloom facility is
          blazing. The light is deliberate — Serrano insisted
          on full-spectrum because the psychological effect
          of adequate lighting on people recovering from
          neural trauma is significant. The facility is the
          brightest place in the undercity. It feels like
          coming indoors.

Smell:    Solder. Coffee (real coffee — Iron Bloom's one
          luxury, sourced through Fex's smuggling network).
          Antiseptic from the clinic. Cooking from the
          quarters. The underlying scent of server hardware
          — warm plastic, ozone, the particular smell of
          fans moving air across circuit boards. Beneath
          everything: the Substrate's mineral warmth, rising
          from below. The facility sits on Substrate-warmed
          ground. The floor is always warm.
```

### 1. ENTRY CORRIDOR
**Past Sable's gate. The resistance welcomes you.**

```
> IRON BLOOM — ENTRY CORRIDOR

The blast door closes behind you. The staircase from
the Fringe levels out into a corridor that was once
a server farm utility passage — cable trays on the
ceiling, ventilation ducts, the ghosts of data
infrastructure. Iron Bloom has made it theirs: the
walls are painted (badly, enthusiastically — a mural
of a gear inside a blooming flower, the Iron Bloom
logo at building scale). String lights run along the
ceiling. A handwritten sign: "YOU MADE IT. WELCOME."

The air is warm and clean — the facility's climate
control is functional, maintained by people who
understand the machinery. After the drainage, the
maintenance tunnels, the transit system's darkness —
this corridor feels like exhaling. The temperature
is comfortable. The lighting is full-spectrum. The
sound is human voices, somewhere ahead, talking about
something that might be important or might be dinner.

The facility begins here. The resistance begins here.
Whatever brought you — evidence, desperation, a name
someone trusted you with — the corridor says: you're
inside now. The hardest part is over.
```

- **Exits:** up (Fringe Ruins — Iron Bloom Entrance, zone 4 r13, through Sable's gate), east (Transit Access), south (The Commons)
- **NPCs:** None (the corridor is a transition — Sable handles security above, the Commons handles reception below)
- **Enemies:** None (the facility interior is a safe zone — the only safe zone in the deep undercity)
- **Objects:**
  - `the_mural` — Examine: "A gear inside a blooming flower. The Iron Bloom logo, painted across the entire corridor wall. The art is sincere — not professional, not polished, but committed. Multiple hands painted this. You can see where styles change mid-petal, where someone corrected someone else's line work, where a child's handwriting added 'WE GROW' in the corner. The mural is a collective self-portrait."
  - `welcome_sign` — Examine: "'YOU MADE IT. WELCOME.' Handwritten. The ink is faded — the sign has been here since the facility opened. It's addressed to everyone who arrives: the defectors, the refugees, the patients, the allies. Everyone who passes through Sable's gate and descends the staircase sees this sign first. It does the thing the surface world forgot how to do: acknowledge that getting here was hard and that being here matters."
  - `warm_floor` — Examine: "The floor is warm. Not heated — the Substrate beneath the facility generates ambient warmth. The server farm was built on bedrock that sits above a Substrate node. The builders didn't know this. Iron Bloom does. The warmth is a gift from whatever lives below. Serrano monitors the Substrate's proximity but doesn't interfere with it. The warmth reduces heating costs. The 33hz vibration is present but gentle — a background hum, not the overwhelming pulse of the transit system's growth areas. The Substrate is close but quiet here. Serrano says it's watching. Not threatening. Watching."


### 3. THE COMMONS
**The heart of the facility. Where the resistance lives.**

```
> IRON BLOOM — THE COMMONS

The server farm's original operations center — a large
room, fifteen meters square, that once held monitoring
consoles and shift supervisors. The consoles are gone.
The room is now a commons: dining tables, a kitchen
area, seating, a library of physical books, and the
indefinable atmosphere of a place where people who
share a purpose gather to be human together.

The light is warm. Full-spectrum LEDs simulate afternoon
sunlight. Someone has hung plants — actual plants,
growing under the LEDs, their leaves reaching toward
the light with the determination of living things in a
place not designed for them. A coffee station occupies
one corner, perpetually active, perpetually surrounded
by people who prioritize caffeine over sleep.

The Commons is where Iron Bloom argues, plans, eats,
and exists. At any given time: engineers debating
signal architecture, a surgeon reviewing patient files,
a new arrival sitting in a corner processing the fact
that they're safe, and someone cooking something that
smells better than it has any right to smell in a
bunker fifty meters underground.
```

- **Exits:** north (Entry Corridor), east (Living Quarters), west (The Clinic), south (Server Farm)
- **NPCs:**
  - `iron_bloom_members` — 4-6 present. Engineers, hackers, medics, refugees. They talk freely — about current projects, about surface conditions, about the food (always about the food). They're the facility's ambient life. Some are long-term residents (years). Some arrived days ago. They differ in background, skill, and opinion, but they share the commons the way a family shares a kitchen.
  - **DOSS** — Operations Manager / Quartermaster (SHOPKEEPER / SERVICES / QUESTGIVER)
    - Location: At the kitchen counter, or at a table with logistics manifests, or anywhere something needs organizing.
    - Faction: Iron Bloom (operations — she keeps the facility running)
    - Disposition: Starts Neutral (0) if the player has Iron Bloom access. Warm. Practical.
    - Personality: Fifties. Solid. She's the person who makes Iron Bloom function — supplies, schedules, morale, the logistics of keeping forty people alive and working underground. She knows where everything is, how much of it remains, and when they'll run out. She knows everyone's name, their dietary restrictions, and their sleep patterns. She's the facility's mother, and she'll tell you she hates that word and then bring you soup.
    - Services:
      - **Facility supplies**: Doss sells equipment, medical supplies, and specialized gear from Iron Bloom's stores. Fair prices. Quality is excellent — Iron Bloom doesn't deal in junk.
      - **Information brokering**: Doss processes all incoming intelligence — deliveries from operatives, data from field assignments, evidence recovered from other zones. She can tell the player what Iron Bloom currently needs and what it currently knows.
      - **Room and board**: Doss manages the living quarters. Iron Bloom allies can rest here — full HP recovery, safe save point, the only deep undercity location with persistent player storage.
    - Quests:
      - **"Supply Run"** (Tier 2): The facility needs specific components — a neural interface calibration kit from Costa's clinic in the Industrial District (z03 r11) and medical-grade anesthetic from Fex's smuggling network (z09 r04). Both are available but neither is easy to transport through hostile territory. The player coordinates pickup and delivery. Reward: CREDS + Iron Bloom reputation + access to advanced equipment in Doss's inventory.
      - **"Morale"** (Tier 2): Something simpler. Doss needs the player to acquire something non-essential but important: music. Physical media. The facility's one portable speaker plays the same twelve albums on loop. People are going crazy. Any physical media — vinyl, tapes, data drives with music — improves morale. A scavenger in the Fringe has a cache. The Freemarket might have imports. The quest is deliberately low-stakes. Not everything is about Helixion. Sometimes the resistance needs a new playlist.
    - Dialogue hook: "You look like you haven't eaten in two days. Sit. — I'll bring you something. While you eat, I'll tell you what we need. While I tell you what we need, you tell me what you've brought. This is how it works here. We share."
- **Enemies:** None
- **Objects:**
  - `the_plants` — Examine: "Growing under full-spectrum LEDs. Trailing vines, a small tomato plant, herbs in repurposed server component trays. The plants shouldn't thrive down here — the depth, the artificial light, the recycled air. They thrive. The Substrate warmth from below, the full-spectrum light from above, and the attention of people who need something green to care for. The plants are Iron Bloom's most human achievement."
  - `the_library` — Examine: "Physical books. Approximately two hundred. Salvaged from the Fringe, donated by arrivals, carried down by people who considered books worth the weight. Technical manuals, fiction, philosophy, poetry. A worn copy of Frankenstein. A medical textbook with Serrano's annotations in the margins. Three different editions of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. The library is small and complete."
  - `coffee_station` — Examine: "Real coffee. Iron Bloom's single luxury. Sourced through Fex's network at significant cost. Doss manages the supply with the intensity of someone who understands that coffee is not about caffeine. It's about the ritual. The act of making coffee is normal. Normal is precious underground. The station is never unattended. Someone is always making coffee. The making is the point."
  - `evidence_wall` — Examine: "One wall of the Commons is covered in documents, photographs, maps, and connecting lines. The evidence against Helixion — compiled from every operative, every defector, every piece of data that's reached the facility. Chrysalis prototype documentation. Assembly Line production data. Broadcast Tower construction timelines. The Voss-Helixion arrangement (if Strand has delivered his documents). The wall is Iron Bloom's case. It's not complete. It's growing."

### 4. LIVING QUARTERS
**Where the resistance sleeps.**

```
> IRON BLOOM — LIVING QUARTERS

The server farm's original storage area — a long, narrow
room with high ceilings — converted into dormitory space.
Bunk beds in rows, each one with a curtain for privacy.
Small personal shelves. Lockers. The beds are made with
varying degrees of care, reflecting the people who sleep
in them: some tight and military, some chaotic, some with
a stuffed animal tucked against the pillow that nobody
mentions.

The quarters accommodate approximately forty people.
Not all beds are occupied — Iron Bloom's population
fluctuates as operatives deploy and return, as patients
heal and leave, as new arrivals find their footing.
The occupancy rate is currently around thirty. Enough
to feel populated. Enough to feel like a community.

A common area at the far end has a couch (salvaged,
comfortable), a table (card games, left mid-hand), and
the single portable speaker that plays the same twelve
albums. The sound carries through the quarters. It's
the last thing people hear before sleep and the first
thing they hear waking. Some find it comforting. Some
are losing their minds.
```

- **Exits:** west (The Commons)
- **NPCs:**
  - **LUX** — Recent Patient (LORE / EMOTIONAL CORE)
    - Location: A bed near the common area. Sitting up, watching the room.
    - Faction: None (civilian — rescued from the Residential Blocks compliance system)
    - Disposition: Starts Quiet (-5). Not hostile — processing. She was brought to Iron Bloom three weeks ago after a compliance clinic visit triggered a neural crisis. Her corporate implant was malfunctioning — sending contradictory signals, causing seizures. The clinic removed the implant. She's healing. It's like removing a voice from your head that's been telling you what to feel.
    - Lux represents what Iron Bloom does in practice. She's the answer to the abstract question of "what is the resistance fighting for?" She says she's learning to make decisions without permission. She says it's the hardest thing she's ever done.

### 5. THE CLINIC
**Where Iron Bloom removes what shouldn't be there.**

```
> IRON BLOOM — THE CLINIC

The room that defines the resistance.

A surgical space converted from a server cooling room —
the climate control was already medical-grade, the
airflow already filtered, the temperature already
precise. Iron Bloom added surgical lighting, an
operating table, monitoring equipment, and the careful
organization of someone who understands that surgery
in a bunker requires more discipline, not less.

The clinic performs two operations. Removal: corporate
implants extracted — compliance modules, kill-switches,
tracking hardware, the mesh interface itself if the
patient chooses. And installation: sovereign
augmentation, built or modified by Iron Bloom engineers,
installed without corporate firmware, without tracking,
without the ability to receive mesh compliance signal.
My body. My blueprint. Not a Chrome Wolf slogan here —
a surgical protocol.

The operating table is occupied. A patient under local
anesthetic, conscious, talking quietly to the surgeon
who works with steady hands and a magnifying headset.
The surgeon narrates the procedure: "Disconnecting
the cortical bridge. You'll feel pressure. Three, two,
one." The patient flinches. The surgeon's hands don't
move. The implant comes out. A small thing. Chrome and
ceramic. It controlled a human being for nine years.
It sits in a tray, inert, the size of a coin.
```

- **Exits:** east (The Commons)
- **NPCs:**
  - **MIRA** — Surgeon / Augmentation Specialist (SERVICES / LORE / QUESTGIVER)
    - Location: At the operating table, or at the sterilization station preparing instruments.
    - Faction: Iron Bloom (chief surgeon — the facility's most critical role after Serrano)
    - Disposition: Starts Neutral (0). Professional. Mira treats everyone the same — with the focused calm of someone whose hands determine whether people live or die.
    - Personality: Late thirties. Precise. She was a neurosurgeon in the city's hospital system before Helixion absorbed it. When the hospital's surgical protocols were modified to include mandatory mesh compliance verification during neural procedures — ensuring no patient left the operating table without a fully functional compliance module — Mira refused. She was terminated. She went underground. Serrano found her.
    - Mira is the facility's most technically skilled person after Serrano, and the most in-demand. She performs 3-4 implant removals per week. Each one is a surgery that, on the surface, would require a full hospital team. She does it with two assistants, salvaged equipment, and the precision of someone who has no margin for error because every patient is someone who trusted her with their brain.
    - She knows about Serrano's condition. She assisted with the self-extraction attempt. She watched Serrano operate on themselves in a mirror and could do nothing because Serrano's hands were in Serrano's own skull and any interference would have been fatal.       - **Implant removal**: Mira removes corporate neural implants — compliance modules, kill-switches, tracking hardware.     - Dialogue hook: "Gloves are on the shelf. If you're going to watch, you're going to be useful. Hold this — steady — don't move until I say. — This is what freedom looks like. It's a surgery. It's small, it's precise, and it changes everything."

### 6. SERVER FARM
**The original infrastructure, repurposed. Processing power for the resistance.**

```
> IRON BLOOM — SERVER FARM

The facility's namesake. Rows of server racks —
original municipal hardware, some still running,
some gutted and replaced with salvaged equipment.
The room is cold — climate control optimized for
hardware, not humans. The hum of cooling fans creates
a white noise that makes the server farm the quietest
room in the facility in terms of human sound. People
come here to think.

Iron Bloom uses the server farm for three purposes.
First: intelligence processing — the computational
power to decrypt intercepted Helixion communications,
analyze mesh traffic patterns, and maintain the
evidence database. Second: counter-signal research —
modeling the 33hz frequency, developing broadcast
interference protocols, and simulating Broadcast Tower
activation scenarios. Third: communication — the server
farm hosts Iron Bloom's encrypted network, connecting
operatives across the city through relay nodes in the
pirate network and the drainage system.
```

- **Exits:** north (The Commons), south (War Room)
- **NPCs:**
  - **CIPHER** — Data Analyst / Intelligence Lead (INFORMANT / QUESTGIVER)
    - Location: At a terminal in the server farm. Multiple screens. Coffee.
    - Faction: Iron Bloom (intelligence — processes all incoming data)

## DESIGN NOTES

**Iron Bloom is the game's home base.** After the hostility, danger, and moral ambiguity of every other zone, Iron Bloom is safe. Full HP recovery. Persistent storage. Full-spectrum lighting. Coffee. The safety is the point — it's what the resistance is fighting to protect. If Iron Bloom is destroyed, the game loses its only safe harbor. The player should feel this threat viscerally because they've experienced what the undercity is like without it.

**Serrano is the game's most complete NPC.** Visionary, dying, the architect of both the weapon and the counter-weapon. The kill-switch self-extraction is the defining detail: Serrano tried to free themselves the way they free others and it went wrong. The person who removes corporate implants couldn't remove their own without paying the price. The tremor is the cost. The degradation is the clock. Every quest in the facility is shaped by the urgency of Serrano's decline — they need things done not because the resistance requires them but because Serrano won't be able to understand the results if they take too long.

**The counter-frequency generator is the game's endgame MacGuffin, earned.** It's not a superweapon. It's a matchbox-sized device that creates a minute of clarity during the mass overwrite. One minute where a million people think for themselves simultaneously. Serrano believes a minute is enough. Coil thinks it's naive. The player carries the generator to the Tower's peak and finds out who's right. The generator works because the Tower's architecture is partly Substrate — the organic material responds to the counter-frequency the way the Substrate responds to everything: with curiosity. The weapon becomes a question again, for sixty seconds.

**Lux is the zone's emotional proof.** She's what Iron Bloom does. A person who was controlled, who is now free, who is struggling with the freedom because the control didn't feel like control. Her description of the mesh experience — warmth, certainty, the voice that tells you everything is fine — is the game's most intimate portrait of compliance. Her description of the removal — cold, doubt, the silence — is the game's most intimate portrait of liberation. Liberation is harder than control. Iron Bloom's mission is to impose that difficulty on an entire city. The ethics of this are not simple.

**Coil's disagreement is the zone's structural tension.** Serrano wants to expose. Coil wants to destroy. Both are correct. Both are insufficient. The game lets the player choose (or combine approaches) through quest alignment. "The Direct Option" delays the Tower. "The Picture" enables the counter-frequency. "The Bridge" deploys it. The ideal path uses all three — Coil's sabotage buys time, Cipher's intelligence enables precision, and Serrano's device creates the window. The disagreement between exposure and action resolves not in one side winning but in both sides being necessary.

**The Deep Lab is the game's most intimate Substrate encounter.** Not the overwhelming presence of the transit Deep Station or the hostile chemistry of the drainage node. A small chamber, a careful excavation, an exposed surface. Touch it: warmth, light, a 14-second response. Serrano's notebook: "I think it's lonely." The Substrate is a living thing that has been beneath the city since before the city existed. It pulses. It responds. It asks. The Deep Lab is where the player, if they've followed the lore through every zone, finally understands what the 33hz has been saying: the earth wants to talk. It doesn't know how. Neither do we.
